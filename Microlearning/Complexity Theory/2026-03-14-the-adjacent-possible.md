## 💡 Concept

**The Adjacent Possible** is a concept from theoretical biologist Stuart Kauffman that describes the set of all things that are one step away from what currently exists. Imagine a room with four doors. Behind each door is a new room with its own doors — but you can only see and open the doors in your current room.

This is how innovation actually works: it doesn't leap from the Stone Age to the iPhone. Each invention opens new doors that weren't previously accessible. The printing press enabled mass literacy, which enabled scientific journals, which enabled peer review, which enabled modern science. Each step was only possible *because* of the previous one.

The power of this idea is that it reframes creativity from "breakthrough genius" to "exploring what's newly reachable." The Adjacent Possible expands every time you step through a door.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

In 2007, YouTube was only possible because five prior developments had recently converged: cheap video cameras, broadband internet, Flash video compression, PayPal (for monetisation infrastructure), and social networking behaviour patterns. None of these were designed *for* YouTube — but their co-existence created an adjacent possible that YouTube stepped into.

Steve Chen and Chad Hurley didn't invent from nothing. They recognised a door that had just appeared. Had they tried the same concept in 2001, they would have failed — the adjacent possible hadn't expanded enough yet.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

**→ Strategy**: This maps directly to the strategic concept of **adjacency moves** in business growth. Chris Zook's research at Bain & Company found that 75% of successful growth initiatives were "adjacency expansions" — not wild diversifications or core repetitions, but deliberate steps into the adjacent possible of the firm's capabilities. The companies that grew sustainably were those that understood which doors were available to them *right now* and which weren't yet.
