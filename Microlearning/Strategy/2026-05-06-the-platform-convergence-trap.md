## 💡 Concept

The Platform Convergence Trap occurs when multiple platforms simultaneously attempt to become the "single pane of glass" for enterprise operations, creating a paradoxical fragmentation rather than consolidation. As AI capabilities mature, every major enterprise platform—from Workday to Salesforce to Microsoft—is positioning itself as the central orchestration layer for business processes. Each platform expands its scope, promising to eliminate the need for others, but this convergent ambition actually creates new integration complexities.

The trap manifests when organizations find themselves with multiple "comprehensive" platforms that overlap significantly but none that truly delivers on the promise of singular control. Instead of reducing system complexity, platform convergence often increases it, as each platform's attempt at comprehensiveness creates new boundary conflicts, data synchronization challenges, and workflow dependencies that didn't exist when systems had clearer, more limited roles.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

Workday's recent positioning as an "AI front door" exemplifies this trap in action. While Workday traditionally focused on HR and financial management, it now promises to be the primary interface for all employee interactions with enterprise systems. Simultaneously, Microsoft is positioning Copilot as the universal AI assistant across all business functions, while Salesforce promotes its Einstein platform as the central nervous system for customer operations.

A Fortune 500 manufacturing company recently discovered this trap when implementing these platforms concurrently. Each system claimed to be the definitive source for employee data, customer interactions, and operational metrics. Rather than achieving the promised simplification, the organization found itself managing complex data flows between three "comprehensive" platforms, each trying to serve as the master system. The result was increased complexity, higher integration costs, and confused end-users unsure which system to use for which tasks.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

This mirrors the Expertise Interference Effect in Learning Science, where multiple expert-level skills can actually impede performance when they overlap in domain coverage. Just as a polyglot might experience language interference when multiple languages compete for cognitive resources during communication, organizations experience "platform interference" when multiple comprehensive platforms compete for the same business processes. The brain's attempt to maintain multiple expert systems creates cognitive switching costs and decision paralysis—the same way enterprises face "platform switching costs" and strategic paralysis when multiple platforms promise identical capabilities through different approaches.
