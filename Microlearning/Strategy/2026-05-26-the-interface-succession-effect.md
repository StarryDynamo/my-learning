## 💡 Concept

The Interface Succession Effect occurs when control over business processes shifts from the organizations that own the underlying systems to those that control the primary user interfaces. As software becomes more abstracted and user-friendly, the most valuable strategic position moves from backend infrastructure ownership to frontend interaction design.

This creates a paradox: the companies that built and maintain the complex systems lose strategic influence to those who simply provide better ways to access them. The effect accelerates when AI becomes the interface layer, as natural language interactions can mask the underlying complexity entirely. Organizations find themselves in a race not just to build better systems, but to control how users interact with those systems—because the interface owner increasingly sets the rules for how value flows through the entire stack.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

Workday's recent positioning as an "AI front door" for enterprise operations exemplifies this effect perfectly. Rather than competing on HR and finance functionality alone, Workday is positioning itself as the primary interface through which employees interact with all enterprise systems. By becoming the conversational layer that sits atop ERP, CRM, and operational systems, Workday could effectively control business workflows regardless of which companies actually process the underlying transactions.

Similarly, when Plaid inserted itself as the interface layer between banks and fintech apps, it gained enormous leverage despite owning no banking infrastructure. The banks held all the deposits and regulatory relationships, but Plaid controlled how developers and consumers accessed that value—making it the strategic chokepoint worth billions.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

In Learning Science, this mirrors the "desirable difficulties" principle—learners often bypass challenging but valuable learning processes by using interfaces that make tasks feel easier. When students use AI writing tools that provide seamless assistance, they may lose the cognitive struggle that builds actual writing competency. The smoothest interface for immediate task completion can undermine the deeper learning that comes from wrestling with complexity. Just as business interfaces can mask strategic dependencies, educational interfaces can mask learning dependencies, creating apparent efficiency while eroding the underlying capabilities that generate long-term value.
