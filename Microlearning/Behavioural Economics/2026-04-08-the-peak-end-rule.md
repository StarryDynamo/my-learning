## 💡 Concept

The Peak-End Rule reveals that people judge an entire experience almost entirely on how they felt at its most intense point (the peak) and how it ended, rather than the total sum or average of every moment. This cognitive bias means that duration has surprisingly little impact on our retrospective evaluations—a phenomenon psychologist Daniel Kahneman called "duration neglect."

What makes this counterintuitive is that our "experiencing self" (living through moments) operates completely differently from our "remembering self" (evaluating experiences afterward). The remembering self dominates our decisions about whether to repeat experiences, recommend them to others, or view them as successful. This explains why a difficult medical procedure with a less painful ending is remembered more favorably than a shorter procedure that ends abruptly at peak discomfort, even though the longer procedure involved more total pain.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

Disney has mastered the Peak-End Rule in their theme park design. Despite long wait times and crowded conditions throughout the day, Disney carefully orchestrates peak moments (meeting beloved characters, spectacular ride finales) and positive endings (fireworks shows, character goodbyes at exit gates). 

In 2019, Disney's customer satisfaction scores remained consistently high at 90%+ despite average wait times of 45-60 minutes per attraction. The company deliberately designs ride experiences to climax near the end—Space Mountain's final spiral, Pirates of the Caribbean's dramatic finale scene—ensuring the most memorable moment occurs just before exit. Even their FastPass system strategically saves guests' best experiences for later in the day. This explains why families spend thousands on Disney vacations and return repeatedly, despite objectively experiencing hours of waiting and physical discomfort.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

Learning Science leverages the Peak-End Rule through strategic experience design. Effective educational programs don't distribute difficulty evenly—they create memorable "aha moments" (peaks) and ensure learners end sessions on successful notes, even if the middle was challenging.

Coding bootcamps exemplify this: they often structure capstone projects to culminate in dramatic demos where students present working applications to industry professionals. This peak moment, combined with graduation ceremonies that emphasize transformation and future potential, creates powerful positive memories that override recollections of months of struggle and confusion. The peak-end design makes graduates enthusiastic ambassadors despite the intense difficulty of the learning process.
