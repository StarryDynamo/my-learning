## 💡 Concept

The Decoy Effect occurs when a deliberately inferior option makes a similar but superior option appear more attractive. This third option—the "decoy"—is asymmetrically dominated: it's clearly worse than one option (the target) but only marginally worse than another (the competitor). The decoy's presence violates our assumption of rational choice independence—adding an option that no one should choose somehow changes preferences between the original two options.

The effect works because we're cognitive misers who struggle with absolute value assessment but excel at relative comparisons. When faced with difficult trade-offs between price and quality, the decoy provides an easy anchor point that makes the target option feel like obvious value. This isn't about manipulation—it's about how our pattern-matching brains naturally process complex choice architectures. The decoy essentially "breaks the tie" in our mental evaluation by providing a reference point that highlights the target's advantages.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

The Economist famously offered three subscription options: web-only for $59, print-only for $125, and web + print for $125. The print-only option was a perfect decoy—identical price to the combo but clearly inferior. MIT students shown all three options overwhelmingly chose the $125 combo (84%). But when shown only web-only ($59) versus combo ($125), most chose the cheaper web-only option (68%). The useless middle option completely flipped preferences.

Dan Ariely's team replicated this across various scenarios. Movie theater popcorn sizes, real estate listings, and even dating profiles all showed the same pattern. The decoy doesn't need to be purchased—it just needs to exist as a reference point. Apple's iPhone pricing follows this pattern religiously, with the mid-tier model often serving as a decoy to make the premium model appear reasonably priced.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

In AI Management, the Decoy Effect appears in model selection and tool adoption decisions. When presenting AI solutions to stakeholders, three options consistently outperform two: a basic solution, an advanced solution, and a deliberately over-engineered "enterprise" option with marginal benefits at significant cost. The expensive decoy makes the advanced solution appear perfectly balanced between capability and cost. Similarly, when choosing between AI models, GPT-4's pricing often serves as a decoy that makes Claude or other alternatives seem more economical, even when the actual use case might not require GPT-4's capabilities. The presence of ultra-high-cost options reshapes how teams evaluate the entire landscape of AI tools.
