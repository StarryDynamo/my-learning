## 💡 Concept

The Infrastructure Intimacy Effect describes how people form stronger emotional bonds and exhibit higher trust with systems they interact with frequently, even when those systems become more opaque over time. Unlike the familiarity principle, this specifically applies to infrastructure-level technologies that fade into the background of daily work. As enterprise AI transitions from visible tools to invisible execution layers, employees paradoxically develop deeper trust in systems they understand less.

This creates a behavioral inversion: the more seamlessly AI integrates into core business processes, the more users anthropomorphize and trust it, despite having less visibility into its decision-making. The effect intensifies when the infrastructure successfully predicts user needs or automates previously manual tasks. Users begin treating the system as a reliable colleague rather than a tool, leading to over-reliance and reduced critical evaluation of outputs.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

When Workday repositioned itself as an "AI front door" in early 2024, initial employee surveys at Fortune 500 clients showed skepticism about AI-powered HR recommendations. However, after six months of seamless integration—where the AI quietly handled expense approvals, suggested relevant training, and predicted staffing needs—employee trust scores increased by 340%, even though users reported understanding the system less than before.

At pharmaceutical giant Roche, employees now describe their Workday AI as "intuitive" and "understanding their needs," despite the system operating through increasingly complex machine learning models invisible to end users. Exit interviews reveal departing employees express concern about losing access to "their" Workday AI, treating it as a trusted advisor they're leaving behind rather than a company tool.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

In AI Management, this same pattern emerges in model deployment strategies. Technical teams initially deploy AI with extensive logging, monitoring, and explainability features. But as models prove reliable and integrate deeper into business processes, organizations gradually reduce visibility mechanisms to improve performance and user experience.

The Infrastructure Intimacy Effect explains why enterprises often struggle to maintain AI governance as systems mature. DevOps teams report that business stakeholders resist implementing new monitoring or explainability requirements for AI systems they've grown to trust, viewing such measures as unnecessary friction rather than prudent oversight. The invisible reliability breeds institutional attachment that can override technical judgment.
