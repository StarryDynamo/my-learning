## 💡 Concept

The Execution Context Trap occurs when AI systems perform brilliantly in isolation but fail catastrophically when integrated into existing business workflows. Unlike simple tool adoption, AI agents require rich contextual information—user permissions, business rules, historical decisions, and organizational constraints—to function effectively. The trap emerges because this context is often implicit, embedded in human judgment and institutional memory rather than explicit system parameters.

Organizations fall into this trap by treating AI integration as a technical challenge rather than an organizational redesign problem. They focus on API connections and data flows while ignoring the contextual scaffolding that makes human decision-making effective. The result is AI that can write perfect emails but sends them to the wrong people, or financial models that are mathematically correct but violate compliance policies the system never learned existed.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

In 2024, a Fortune 500 manufacturing company deployed an AI procurement agent to automate supplier negotiations. The system excelled in testing, reducing costs by 15% while maintaining quality standards. However, when rolled out enterprise-wide, it created a crisis by renegotiating contracts with strategic partners during a sensitive merger negotiation, violating unwritten protocols about supplier relationships during M&A activity.

The AI had access to procurement databases and cost optimization algorithms but lacked context about broader corporate strategy, relationship sensitivities, and timing considerations that human procurement managers intuitively understood. The company spent six months rebuilding not just the AI system, but codifying the implicit organizational knowledge required for contextually appropriate decision-making—context that had never needed to be explicit before.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

In Learning Science, this mirrors the "Transfer Problem"—the difficulty students face applying classroom knowledge to real-world situations. Students can solve textbook physics problems perfectly but struggle with the same principles in unfamiliar contexts because academic problems strip away the contextual complexity of real situations.

Both phenomena reveal that expertise isn't just about knowing rules or algorithms—it's about understanding when and how to apply them given situational nuances. Just as educators must design learning experiences that bridge classroom-to-world contexts, AI managers must architect systems that capture and utilize the rich contextual embedding where AI agents will actually operate.
