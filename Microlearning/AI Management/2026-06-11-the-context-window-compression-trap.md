## 💡 Concept

The Context Window Compression Trap occurs when organizations compress complex business processes into AI prompts, inadvertently discarding critical contextual nuances that humans naturally preserve. As enterprises move AI from experimentation to core operations, managers face pressure to standardize workflows into prompt templates and API calls. However, this compression often strips away the subtle environmental cues, historical context, and interpersonal dynamics that experienced workers intuitively factor into decisions.

The trap manifests when AI systems produce technically correct but contextually inappropriate outputs because the compressed instructions lack the rich situational awareness that guided human decision-making. Unlike simple automation that replaces clearly defined tasks, AI systems require context to make nuanced judgments—but the very act of formalizing that context for AI consumption can inadvertently destroy its most valuable elements. Organizations fall into this trap by treating AI integration as a simple input-output optimization problem rather than a context preservation challenge.

## 🔍 Real-World Example

Workday's new "AI front door" strategy illustrates this trap in action. While positioning itself as the central interface for enterprise AI interactions, early implementations have struggled with context compression. For instance, when JPMorgan Chase integrated AI into their employee service requests through Workday in late 2025, the system initially generated responses that were policy-compliant but ignored department-specific cultural norms and individual employee histories.

A request for flexible work arrangements would receive standardized responses based on official policy, missing crucial context like the employee's previous performance reviews, team dynamics, or department-specific precedents that human HR representatives naturally considered. The AI correctly processed the formal request parameters but lost the contextual richness that made human responses more effective. Chase had to spend months rebuilding context awareness by creating detailed employee and department profiles, essentially reconstructing the informal knowledge that was compressed out during initial AI integration.

## 🌉 Cross-Domain Bridge

This mirrors the "Desirable Difficulties" principle in Learning Science, where removing contextual complexity from learning environments actually impairs long-term retention and transfer. Educational researchers found that overly simplified, "efficient" learning materials strip away the contextual cues that help students apply knowledge in novel situations.

Just as educators learned that some friction and complexity in learning materials improves outcomes, AI managers must resist the urge to over-simplify business processes for AI consumption. The messiness and ambiguity that seem inefficient are often carrying crucial contextual information. Both domains reveal that optimization for immediate clarity can undermine long-term effectiveness by destroying the very complexity that enables adaptive responses.
